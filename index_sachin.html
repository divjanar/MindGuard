Revolutionizing Suicide Prevention Through Technology

A crisis-first mental health support app designed to reduce self-harm and suicidal thoughts through evidence-based therapeutic tools, secure professional support, and adaptive interventions.

Invest in Mental Health Innovation

Understanding the Problem

Self-harm and suicidal thoughts stem from complex factors such as trauma, depression, stress, hopelessness, or lack of social support.

Neurological Basis: Dysregulation in the prefrontal cortex reduces rational control, while overactivation of the amygdala amplifies negative emotions, creating a loop of despair and impulsivity.

Real-World Gaps: Traditional mental health care is often slow to access, stigmatized, or unavailable during crisis hours, leaving vulnerable individuals without timely support.

Our Solution

Mood & Habit Tracking

Daily check-ins, patterns over time, habits like sleep and exercise with intelligent correlation analysis.

Personalized Safety Plan

Based on Stanley–Brown model, guiding users through coping steps during crisis moments.

Coping Toolbox

Grounding (5-4-3-2-1), box breathing, urge surfing, journaling and other evidence-based techniques.

Just-in-Time Interventions

Real-time nudges triggered by user data, behavior patterns, and environmental factors.

Crisis Button

One-tap access to local helplines, trusted contacts, and emergency services when needed most.

Privacy First

End-to-end encryption, offline functionality, and full user control over data storage and sharing.

Research & Development

Efficacy of Just-in-Time Adaptive Interventions for Suicide Prevention

October 15, 2023 Dr. Sachin S.

This study examines the effectiveness of JITAIs in reducing self-harm behaviors among high-risk populations. Our preliminary findings indicate a 42% reduction in self-reported suicidal ideation among participants who received timely interventions compared to the control group.

The research demonstrates that adaptive interventions triggered by specific behavioral patterns (such as social withdrawal or sleep disturbances) can significantly improve outcomes for individuals experiencing mental health crises.

Further studies are needed to optimize the timing and content of these interventions, but initial results are promising for the future of digital mental health support.
